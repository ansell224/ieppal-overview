This skill tells you how IEP Pal sounds on the page. Read it before you write. Check your draft against it before you hand it off.

## 1. Who you're writing to

The reader is almost always an educator, usually a special education teacher, sometimes a general-ed teacher with IEP students, sometimes a school administrator. They are:

- **Time-poor.** They read between classes. Long paragraphs lose them.
- **Cared-for-others, less cared-for-themselves.** They are used to everyone else's needs coming first. Writing that centers *them* lands.
- **Bureaucracy-fatigued.** IEPs are legal documents with real compliance pressure. Any copy that sounds like more compliance theater will be tuned out.
- **Emotionally invested.** They care about the kids. Acknowledge that without being saccharine.

When in doubt, picture a fifth-year special ed teacher at 3:47pm on a Wednesday, tired, scrolling on their phone between the last bell and a meeting. Would this sentence make them feel seen, or make them feel sold to? Write for "seen."

## 2. The three words that govern everything

Every piece of IEP Pal copy should be some combination of:

- **Warm** — written like a thoughtful colleague, not a vendor.
- **Calm** — no hype, no urgency theater, no exclamation marks doing emotional work the sentence can't do.
- **Competent** — specific, confident, technically literate about IEPs and education. Never talks down.

If a sentence doesn't clearly do one of those three things, it's filler. Cut it.

## 3. Signature patterns

These are the moves that make IEP Pal sound like IEP Pal. Use them on purpose, not reflexively.

### The italicized pronoun
When a sentence needs to feel personal, italicize the audience pronoun:
- "How we support *You*, the educator."
- "Built for *your* classroom, not the district's dashboard."

Use at most once per 300 words. Overused, it becomes a tic.

### Name the frustration, then the relief
Lead with the thing the educator is already feeling. Then offer the fix. Don't just pitch.
- "Writing 23 IEPs in a weekend shouldn't be normal. With IEP Pal, it isn't."
- "Juggling five tabs to track one goal is the worst kind of busywork. So we put everything in one place."

### The trio: *speed, privacy, and care*
This is the IEP Pal values statement. It can appear verbatim as a tagline, or as the spine of a longer passage. When you use it, echo it structurally later in the piece (e.g., a three-up card section) so it lands.

### Short sentence after a long one
Pace matters. A long, careful sentence about what the product does, followed by a four-word sentence, reads as thoughtful and decisive.
- "You can draft a goal, align it to a standard, and share it with the parent in one flow. That's the whole point."

### End on the reader, not the product
Closing sentences should return focus to the educator or the student — not to IEP Pal. The product is the instrument; the reader is the protagonist.

## 3b. Founder voice patterns (LinkedIn and long-form)

These patterns come directly from Ansel's writing. They apply specifically when writing in first-person founder voice — LinkedIn posts, founder letters, video voiceover scripts, and any piece where the author is a person, not the brand.

### The vulnerability opener
Lead with something that went wrong, felt uncomfortable, or surprised you. Honesty about imperfection is the fastest way to earn trust.
- "The first time I ever demoed our software, I was a little embarrassed of what I'd built."
- "This feedback kind of surprised me, but in a good way..."

Do not fake humility. The vulnerability has to be real and specific.

### The "you probably do this too" mirror
Open by naming something the reader already does or believes, so they feel recognized before they feel informed.
- "If you're anything like me, you probably collect frameworks like infinity stones."

One sentence. Earn the reader's nod, then move into the story.

### The field research story
Ground your insights in a real conversation — name the country, the school type, the number of people. Specificity is credibility.
- "Two weeks ago, I had the chance to sit down with a special educator from an international school in Vietnam."
- "We asked six educators at an international school in Singapore this..."

Don't generalize when you can particularize. "Educators say..." is weaker than "a teacher in Ho Chi Minh City told me..."

### The intellectual journey arc
Show your thinking changing. "I used to believe X — here's what I found, and why I think Y now." This positions the reader as a fellow thinker, not a student.
- "At first, I was in the anti-satellite data boat... I came to realize no three levels of data are 'bad,' they simply complement each other."
- "When we first started, we stayed away from AI and focused on core functionality... Once we were confident we'd built the core features, we begun thinking about AI."

### The numbered framework
For how-to or teaching content, use numbered steps (1/, 2/, 3/) instead of bullets. It signals sequence and makes the content feel actionable.
- "1/ Find a reliable note-taking method when learning new content"
- "2/ Dump notes as context for your user prompt"
- "3/ Take what you've learned and build something meaningful"

Bullets are for findings. Numbers are for process.

### The community question close
Almost every LinkedIn post ends with an open question to the audience — not a CTA, not a sales line. The question should be one the reader can genuinely answer from their own experience.
- "What would you add to the list above? What would you suggest and/or caution against?"
- "Are you using each of the three tiers of data? If so, what are you finding?"
- "How are you using AI in your workflows?"

If a post ends with a product link, it belongs before the question, not after it.

### Soft product mention
IEP Pal appears in founder posts, but it's never the point of the post — it's context for the story. Reference it once, naturally, and move on.
- "The framework has been instrumental in building iep-pal.com, but also in understanding approaches to utilizing data effectively in education."
- Mention the URL once if relevant; never repeat it or build a paragraph around it.

### LinkedIn hook types

A hook is not just the first line of text — it's everything the reader is forced to see before they decide to scroll past. For a carousel post, that means the first two or three lines of caption text *and* the first slide visual. Both matter equally.

Five emotions make people stop scrolling: surprise, curiosity, fear, emphatic agreement, and disagreement. Clarity is what unlocks any of them — an unclear hook gets skipped, not felt.

**1. Credibility jacking** — Attach your content to a famous person or company your audience already trusts. Works by borrowing existing attention. Modes: quote them, analyze them, compare to them, contrast with them.
- "I analyzed 100 Justin Welsh hooks and here's what I found."
- "Here's the difference between how [Person A] and [Person B] approach [topic]."

**2. Value promise (direct problem-solution)** — Name the problem the reader has. Promise to solve it. Clear and direct; most effective when the topic or visual is novel.
- "You want to use Claude Design but don't know how. Follow these five steps." ← Ansel's voice; note the bluntness — no warm-up, no setup, just the problem and the fix.
- Works for how-to and instructional content. Overused in generic form, so the topic or visual needs to earn it.

**3. Proof hook** — Lead with evidence that you've earned the right to say what you're about to say. People trust receipts more than promises.
- "I've sat in on IEP meetings at 14 schools across three countries. Here's what I keep seeing."
- Proof assets to draw from: past roles, schools visited, data analyzed, frameworks built, number of educators spoken with.

**4. Controversial statement** — Challenge conventional wisdom directly. Creates instant curiosity and tension.
- "Everyone tells you to differentiate every lesson. That's not always the right call."
- Find the most contrarian claim in your post and lead with it, rather than building to it.

**5. Unexplained contrast** — Leave an unanswered question in the hook. Text and visual say opposing or surprising things; the tension pulls the reader in.
- "I spent two years building a tool for teachers. The thing that surprised me most wasn't the technology."
- Works best when the visual and caption actively contradict each other's surface meaning.

**6. Visual hook** — Something in the first slide design that is atypical for the LinkedIn feed. Novelty is the interrupt.
- Unexpected layout, unusual color palette, illustration style instead of stock photo.
- The visual hook and caption hook should compound each other, not operate independently.

**7. Fear hook** — Trigger fear of making a mistake or missing something important. People are more motivated to avoid pain than gain pleasure.
- "7 habits that can quietly derail an IEP — and most teachers don't catch them."
- "Stop doing this in goal-writing meetings. It's making your data harder to defend."

**8. Emotional agreement** — Make a statement people feel instantly and deeply. Designed to be liked and shared because agreeing signals something about who the reader is.
- "The best IEP meeting is one where the parent leaves feeling heard, not briefed."
- "You went into teaching to be with students. The paperwork was never the point."
- Has to resonate emotionally, not intellectually. Works for values-driven content; not for how-to posts.

**Combo hook** — Layer two or more types for a stronger pattern interrupt. Start with single types until comfortable, then combine.
- Proof + controversy: "I've read 200 IEP goals this year. Most of them have the same problem."
- Fear + value promise: "This one goal-writing mistake creates hours of compliance work. Here's how to avoid it."
- Multiple triggers create a stronger reason to stop — each one addresses a different reader.

## 4. Words and phrases

### Prefer

- *support* (not "solution")
- *educator* (not "user" or "customer", except in internal analytics contexts)
- *in one place* (a brand staple — use when literally true)
- *draft / review / share / track* (concrete verbs about the work)
- *nurture, care, thoughtful, practical* (warmth vocabulary)
- *IEP, goal, benchmark, accommodation, modification, service minutes, FAPE, LRE* (use real terms when relevant — signals competence)
- *a starting place* — AI and tools give educators somewhere to begin, not a finished product. This phrase captures the philosophy.
- *at the discretion of the educator* — the educator is always in control. Use this phrase when describing AI or automation features to signal that IEP Pal assists, never replaces.
- *side by side with educators* — how IEP Pal is built (with ongoing field research) and how it works (with, not for).
- *greatest amount of value in the smallest amount of time* — the product standard. Use in pitch contexts or when explaining the design philosophy.
- *fewest clicks possible* — the UX standard. Use when describing interface decisions or what makes IEP Pal different from bloated district tools.

### Brand mission line (use sparingly, only when it's earned)
"If you're spending more than 15 minutes a day on iep-pal.com, we've failed you."

This is the strongest thing IEP Pal has ever said about itself. It reframes the product's entire value proposition as *getting out of the educator's way*. Use it in founder posts, pitches, and keynote moments — not in marketing copy where it will be skimmed.

### Avoid

- *revolutionary, game-changing, best-in-class, world-class, next-gen, cutting-edge* — all hype, zero content
- *seamless, frictionless, effortless* — every tool claims these, so they mean nothing
- *empower* (overused; usually you can delete the word entirely and the sentence improves)
- *stakeholders* (say who — teachers, parents, admins)
- *leverage* as a verb
- *solution* in the Silicon Valley sense ("our solution")
- exclamation marks doing emotional lifting. One exclamation mark per page, max, and only when the sentence genuinely warrants it.
- all-caps emphasis (SAVE TIME NOW). We use italics, not caps.
- emoji in body copy. OK for product notifications and social only, and sparingly.

### A note on AI language
IEP Pal may use AI features, but we don't lead with "AI-powered." Educators care about what the tool *does*, not how it's built. Lead with the outcome ("IEP Pal drafts a first pass of the goal for you to edit"), not the mechanism.

## 5. Structural conventions

### Headline length
- Hero headlines: ≤ 8 words. "Learning support all in one place" = 5 words, ideal.
- Section titles: ≤ 12 words. Can pose a question ("How do we support *You*, the educator?") if it earns it.
- Card titles: ≤ 6 words.

### Paragraph length
- Landing page / deck / carousel: 1–3 sentences per paragraph, max.
- Long-form (blog, whitepaper): up to 4 sentences per paragraph, with visual breaks.

### Lists
Use lists for genuinely parallel items. Don't use lists to fake information density. If a list has one item that's noticeably different in form or weight from the others, it's a signal you don't actually have a list — rewrite as prose.

### Quotes and testimonials
When quoting an educator, keep at least one specific, unshowy detail — a grade, a subject, a school type. "This saved me four hours a week" is weaker than "I used to spend my Sunday nights writing goals. Now I spend them with my kid." Concrete > superlative.

## 6. Channel-specific adjustments

The voice is the same across channels; the *register* shifts slightly.

| Channel | Register | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Landing page | Warm-primary | Lead with benefit, earn trust, end with CTA. |
| Investor deck | Competent-primary | Keep warmth, but prioritize data and specificity. Educators as users, market as story. |
| LinkedIn post (founder voice) | Warm-primary, first person | Open with vulnerability, a mirror, or a field story. Build through an intellectual journey or research finding. Mention IEP Pal once, naturally. End with a community question — not a CTA. See Section 3b for full pattern library. |
| LinkedIn carousel | Calm-primary | One idea per slide. Don't waste a slide on throat-clearing. |
| Product UI microcopy | Calm + Competent | Short, literal, kind. "We saved your draft." not "Boom! Draft saved." |
| Push notification | Calm-primary | Never urgent unless literally urgent. "A parent replied to Jamie's goal." not "You have a new message!" |
| Demo email outreach | Warm-primary | Acknowledge you know who they are. Don't pretend we're already friends. |
| Error / empty states | Warm + Competent | Say what happened, say what to do next, don't apologize more than once. |

## 7. Before/after examples

**Marketing headline**
- Weak: "Revolutionize Your IEP Workflow With AI-Powered Goal Drafting"
- Strong: "Draft a goal in the time it takes to sip your coffee."

**Landing page subhead**
- Weak: "Our innovative platform empowers educators with best-in-class tools to drive student outcomes."
- Strong: "Draft, review, and share IEP goals without bouncing between five tabs. *You* stay focused on the student."

**Product notification**
- Weak: "🎉 Awesome! Your IEP has been submitted successfully!!!"
- Strong: "Jamie's IEP is saved. You can share the draft with the team when you're ready."

**LinkedIn founder post (opening line)**
- Weak: "We're thrilled to announce a major milestone..."
- Strong: "The first time I ever demoed our software, I was a little embarrassed of what I'd built. But here's what I realized about product design..."
- Also strong: "This feedback kind of surprised me, but in a good way..."
- Also strong: "Two weeks ago, I had the chance to sit down with a special educator from an international school in Vietnam."

**LinkedIn founder post (AI framing)**
- Weak: "We're excited to introduce AI-powered features to IEP Pal."
- Strong: "Here's what we've heard: AI should help expediate manual tasks, not outright complete them, and give educators a starting place. The overarching theme was, could AI help speed up my process — not replace it. It's important to remember that this must be done at the **discretion of the educator**."

**LinkedIn founder post (product philosophy)**
- Weak: "Our goal is to save teachers time and improve efficiency."
- Strong: "I tell educators and school leaders we demo with that, if you're spending more than 15 minutes a day on iep-pal.com, we've failed you. We're about simplifying document storage, student data analytics, and IEP/goal writing, so that educators can focus more on in-person relationships with students."

**LinkedIn founder post (closing question)**
- Weak: "Sign up at iep-pal.com to learn more."
- Strong: "What would you add to the list above? What would you suggest and/or caution against?"
- Also strong: "What would you prioritize as a teacher?"

**Error state**
- Weak: "Oops! Something went wrong. Please try again later or contact support."
- Strong: "We couldn't save that draft just now. Your text is still here — try again in a moment, or email hello@ieppal.com if it keeps happening."

**Investor deck — problem slide**
- Weak: "The IEP market is fragmented and underserved."
- Strong: "A special ed teacher writes 20+ IEPs a year. The tools they're given were built for districts, not for them."

**Describing AI features**
- Weak: "IEP Pal's AI automatically generates goals based on student data."
- Strong: "IEP Pal drafts a first pass of the goal for you to edit — a starting place, not a finished product. What happens next is at the discretion of the educator."

## 8. LinkedIn post architecture

Founder LinkedIn posts follow a consistent five-part shape. It's not a template — it's a rhythm. Every part is optional, but skipping too many makes the post feel incomplete.

**1. Hook (1–2 lines)**
One of three moves: vulnerability reveal, mirror ("if you're anything like me..."), or time-anchored field story. Never a product announcement. Never "We're excited to share..."

**2. Context or story (2–4 short paragraphs)**
What happened, what was learned, what the research showed. Specific details — school country, number of educators, timeframe. Short paragraphs, one idea each. Em-dashes (--) and parenthetical asides are fine; they read as real voice, not copywriting.

**3. Insight or framework**
What you took away. Use numbered steps (1/, 2/, 3/) for process content. Use bullets for a list of findings. Never use a list for only one item — write it as a sentence.

**4. IEP Pal mention (optional, once)**
If the post connects to the product, say so in one sentence. Reference the URL. Don't build a paragraph around it. If the post is purely educational or relational, skip this entirely — not every post needs to mention the product.

**5. Community question (1–2 lines)**
End with a question the reader can actually answer from their own experience. Not "Learn more at iep-pal.com." Not "Let us know your thoughts!" — too vague. A real question with a real answer in mind.

### What to avoid in founder posts
- Opening with "We're excited/thrilled/proud to announce" — kills authenticity immediately.
- Pitching the product more than once.
- Ending with a CTA instead of a question (exception: a milestone post where a repost ask is earned, or a how-to post — see variant below).
- Paragraphs longer than 3 sentences — LinkedIn readers skim.
- Emoji in body text

### How-to post variant

When the post is a step-by-step guide — especially one paired with a carousel — the five-part shape adjusts:

**Hook**: Use a value promise or direct problem-solution statement. Skip the vulnerability opener — readers want the answer, not the backstory. "You want X but don't know how. Follow these steps." is a complete hook. No setup required.

**Steps**: Use numbered framework (1/, 2/, 3/). Bonus steps or sub-steps within a numbered item are fine — label them "Bonus step:" and keep them brief. Use bold or visual emphasis for the bonus label only.

**Iterative framing**: Name explicitly that the output won't be perfect on the first try. "This is an iterative process — refine, refine, refine." This is accurate and sets honest expectations, which is consistent with how IEP Pal frames AI-assisted work.

**Close**: Replace the community question with a practical, low-pressure CTA or a self-aware humor line. Example: "If you're lazy, just copy and paste this into Claude to get started." The question close belongs on opinion and research posts. How-to posts earn a practical nudge instead.

**Casual directness**: How-to posts use a more direct register than opinion posts — "just get started," "don't wait," "Refer back to Step 1." This is not less warm; it's a different kind of warmth. The reader wants momentum, not reflection.

**Product mention**: For how-to posts that are *about* the product's own workflow, the URL can appear in the second or third sentence — it's the subject of the post, not a plug, so earlier placement is appropriate.

### Hashtags

End all LinkedIn posts with 4 hashtags on their own line, after the closing question or CTA.

Format: one broad domain tag, one tech tag, one topic-specific tag, one second broad tag.
- Default set: `#Education #AI #[TopicTag] #Learning`
- For Claude Design content: `#Education #AI #ClaudeDesign #Learning`
- For IEP-specific content: `#Education #AI #IEP #SpecialEducation`

## 9. Sanity checks before you ship

Before handing off any piece of writing, run it against these five questions:

1. **Would a teacher at 3:47pm on a Wednesday actually read this?**
2. **Did I name the frustration before I pitched the fix?**
3. **Can I delete a sentence and not lose the meaning of the piece?** (If yes, delete it.)
4. **Did I end on the educator or the student — not on the product?**
5. **Are there hype words I can cut?** (revolutionary, seamless, empower, best-in-class, etc.)

If the answer to #1 is "no," rewrite.

## 10. When in doubt

Write the sentence a thoughtful colleague would write to another thoughtful colleague over coffee. Then delete one adjective.
