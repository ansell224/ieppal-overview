## Who Ansel is (one-paragraph anchor)

Ansel Natarajan is a British-Indian Singaporean co-founder of IEP Pal, an edu-management platform built for special education teachers. He co-founded IEP Pal straight out of high school in mid-2024, while simultaneously serving two years of compulsory National Service (NS) in the Singapore Armed Forces. He's 19, building a B2B SaaS startup from a military back-office role, and studying business online at Penn State. His lens: what it means to build technology *side by side with* educators, not for them.

---

## Founder origin story

**The spark — a classroom consultancy project.**
In his final year of high school at Singapore American School, Ansel worked as a teaching assistant in QUEST, an interdisciplinary program. A student-led consultancy project for the school surfaced a specific problem: there was no single intuitive platform for IEPs. Teachers were disengaged, students unaware of their own plans, parents barely involved. He and his co-founder decided not to wait for someone else to build it.

**Field research — 400+ educator interviews.**
After graduating in May 2024, before any product existed, Ansel independently contacted over 400 educators across Hong Kong, Denmark, Cambodia, Holland, Singapore, and Vietnam to understand what an ideal IEP platform would actually achieve. This is the proof point that separates IEP Pal from tools built in a vacuum.

**The prototype.**
Launched in October 2024 — a working product, not a deck — tested with real teachers.

## Key narrative threads

These are the through-lines that tie proof points into stories. Pick one thread per post — don't load multiple at once.

**1. Building in the margins.**
Building a startup while serving NS. The constraint (back-office role, limited hours) became the discipline engine. Every hour of product work was carved out. Use when the topic is about focus, prioritization, or founder grit.

**2. Field research before code.**
400+ educators before a single line of product code. The product is a response to what real teachers said, not a guess. Use when the topic is about listening to users, EdTech, IEPs, or the gap between tools and teachers.

**3. Trust built in the field and in the jungle.**
SAF stories: sleeping in a hole in the ground, assuming command when the section leader was "shot," covering fire in the dark. These are not metaphors — they're the actual texture of Ansel's leadership training. Use when the topic is about trust, leadership under pressure, or decision-making.

**4. The rarity of a young founder doing primary research.**
Most people Ansel's age are pitching ideas. He went and talked to 400 teachers first. Use when the topic is about how to validate ideas, entrepreneurship, or the gap between ambition and evidence.

**5. Education is personal.**
His mother is a professor at NUS. He grew up around education as a vocation, not just a sector. Use when the topic is about why EdTech matters or the stakes of getting it right.

## Voice guardrails specific to Ansel's story

- **Never inflate the age angle.** "Young founder" as a hook works once. After that it's a crutch. Let the work speak.
- **Specificity over superlatives.** "400 educators in six countries" beats "extensive global research."
- **The NS detail humanizes, not victimizes.** He's not suffering through service — he's working in parallel. Frame it as constraint that shaped focus, not hardship.
- **The Serendipity Engine is context, not distraction.** Mention it when it's genuinely relevant (marketing, coaching, content creation). Don't try to tie both startups into every post.
- **IEP Pal appears once.** Per the founder voice guide — it's context for the story, never the point of the post.
