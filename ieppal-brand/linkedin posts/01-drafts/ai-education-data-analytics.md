Special education teachers have always had the data.

The problem was never collection — it was comprehension.

Before I wrote a single line of code for IEP Pal, I spoke with 400+ educators across Singapore, Vietnam, Hong Kong, Cambodia, Denmark, and Holland. The same pattern kept surfacing: teachers weren't in a data void. They were drowning in data *without context*.

Progress notes scattered across three platforms. Goals written in September, revisited in April. Benchmark scores living in a spreadsheet no one had time to interpret. A student's full picture — their patterns, their momentum, their gaps — existed somewhere. It just wasn't readable at 7pm on a Tuesday.

This is what AI is actually changing in education right now. Not the content generation. The *interpretation layer*.

AI and learning analytics are making it possible for a teacher to surface a student's progress trend across a semester in seconds — and act on it before it becomes a compliance problem. A recent scoping review on AI and educational data analytics in K–12 IEP implementation found direct improvements in progress monitoring and intervention fidelity. Nearly 60% of special education teachers reported using AI for IEP development in the last school year — an 18-point jump in a single year.

That number surprised me. What didn't surprise me was *why*: it's not that AI is writing their IEPs for them. It's that AI is finally making their existing data legible.

At IEP Pal (iep-pal.com), we think about this constantly — how to surface the right data, at the right time, at the discretion of the educator. Not to replace the judgment a teacher has built over years. To give that judgment something to work with.

How are you seeing data analytics show up in your classroom or school? What's working — and what still feels like noise?
