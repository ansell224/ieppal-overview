I was watching SpaceX's Starship documentary last week and I kept noticing an assumption buried underneath all of it.

Mars is the plan. It's been the plan so long it barely reads as a choice anymore. So on a Monday morning I asked myself a different question: what would it actually take to terraform Venus instead?

Not because Venus is easy — it's the opposite. 460°C surface. Atmospheric pressure 93x Earth's. The most extreme greenhouse effect in the solar system.

But strip that away, and Venus is almost exactly Earth's twin. 90% of our surface gravity. 95% of our radius. Inside the habitable zone.

The path to making it habitable involves a sunshade made of angled reflective rings (which holds itself in position through geometry, not propulsion), CO2 literally raining out of the sky as a liquid once temperature and pressure hit the right point, ice ferried from Europa where the kinetic energy of each delivery simultaneously removes CO2 from the atmosphere — every step follows physics we already understand. Nothing here requires a breakthrough.

The bottleneck isn't technical. It's generational.

The engineers who laid the foundations of medieval cathedrals never saw the spires. The planners of Rome's aqueducts built deliberately for people they'd never meet. They did it anyway.

That's the question underneath all of this — not "can we get there," but what does it look like to build something bigger than one human life?
