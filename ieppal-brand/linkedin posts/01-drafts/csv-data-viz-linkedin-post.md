# LinkedIn post — CSV data visualization launch

Four hook variants of the same post. Each ~220–250 words. Closes on a community question per voice guide §3b. Feedback source kept generic ("educators told us…") per Ansel's direction.

## Version 1 — Field research story hook

When we first shipped data visualization in IEP Pal, we built it the way we thought made sense. Open a chart, type your data in, pick a chart type, ship it. Educators told us it worked — and that it took too long.

That was the part we didn't expect.

Manual entry felt like a feature, not a friction point. Total control over every value. But control isn't worth much when you've got 40 minutes of prep period and a parent meeting in an hour.

So we rebuilt it.

The new flow is four steps:

1/ Drag a CSV into the page.
2/ Pick a chart type.
3/ Adjust your axes and titles.
4/ Done.

Most educators already have the data sitting somewhere — a progress monitoring sheet, a district export, a Google Sheet they've been quietly maintaining since September. The chart is built off what they already have, not what they retype.

We try to hold ourselves to the fewest clicks possible. This one earned its place.

What's the data task that eats the most of your week — and is it the entering, the formatting, or figuring out what to show?

## Version 2 — Vulnerability / surprise hook

This feedback surprised me, in a good way.

When we shipped data visualization in IEP Pal, I was quietly proud of the manual entry. You could type in any value, pick any chart, and get something share-ready. I thought we'd nailed it.

Educators told us we hadn't.

The manual entry was helpful — they kept using that word, "helpful" — but it took too long. And "helpful but slow" is a polite way of saying "I won't use this on a Wednesday."

So we asked what they actually wanted. The answer was almost embarrassingly simple: just let me drop my file in.

The new flow:

1/ Drag a CSV into the page.
2/ Pick a chart type.
3/ Adjust your axes and titles.
4/ Done.

Most educators already have the data — progress monitoring exports, a Google Sheet they've been keeping since September, district platform downloads. The chart builds off what's already there. Nothing gets retyped.

The lesson I keep relearning: a feature is only as useful as the time it costs to use it. "Helpful but slow" is a failure mode we should take as seriously as a bug.
