My default when someone brings me a problem is to jump straight to solutions.

I think I'm being helpful, and sometimes I am; but, solutions aren't always what is immediately needed.

A few weeks ago, I participated in a workshop with [Growthbeans](https://www.linkedin.com/feed#) at #CoachUP9 that introduced me to the NASA model. Here's a breakdown:

Notice: These are the words someone uses, their tone, their body language.

Acknowledge: This may sound like: "I can't imagine how difficult that was to hear." Help the person feel like their experiences are real and valid.

Share: Verbalize what you noticed. A bit of encouragement fits here too, if it's genuine.

Ask: Questions that help someone arrive at their own solutions. They're more likely to follow through because they suggested it.

What I keep returning to is that N and A in particular require something I often underestimate: undivided presence. It's hard to do that if your mind is moving at a million miles an hour.

It's worth mentioning that not every conversation calls for this. Sometimes people just need instructions, but the skill is knowing the difference.

When I think of iep-pal.com this is exactly the stuff I envision educators spending time learning, instead of sitting in front of an excel sheet trying to figure it out.
