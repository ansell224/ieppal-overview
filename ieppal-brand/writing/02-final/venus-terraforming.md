Something about the Artemis II mission recently made me stop and think about what it actually means to become a multiplanetary species. Not the mission itself (though that's remarkable on its own terms) but the longer question it raises: if we're going to leave Earth, what does that actually look like? Where do we go, and what could civilization look like when we get there?

I've been following [Marcus House's coverage of the Starship program lately](https://youtu.be/n59byrpGy48?si=apuQkldavOlGEMxV), and my fascination has only grown. SpaceX released a [short documentary](https://youtu.be/ANe_HW4X8oc?si=Y3ssjiL_XJGTvZvP) recently about Starship's testing progress since their last launch in November 2025, and what struck me watching it was the assumption underneath it: that Mars is the ultimate destination. NASA's long-term planning points there and SpaceX's entire architecture is building for it.

Mars is, in the popular imagination, the next step for humanity; however, what if for a moment we turned our attention elsewhere. Mars might be the right instinct, but what would it take to become multiplanetary, not just in the sense of flags and footprints, but in the sense of a second civilization, with billions of people, ecosystems, cities, a future?

The last time any human-made object reached the surface of Venus was in early 1982, when the Soviet probes Venera 13 and 14 landed there. We haven't sent anything back since, but for a long time before that mission, the scientific community was genuinely divided about what Venus was. Beneath its thick, permanent cloud cover, some scientists believed there might be something close to a utopia: warm, perhaps humid, possibly hospitable. Carl Sagan argued that those clouds were actually trapping solar heat in a runaway greenhouse effect, making the surface completely uninhabitable. He was right. Venus's atmosphere is 97% CO2, its surface sits at around 460°C, and its atmospheric pressure is 93 times that of Earth. Safe to say, a spacesuit is not optional.

What stayed with me after reading all of this was a simpler fact. Venus is almost the same size as Earth: 90% of our surface gravity, 95% of our radius, 81% of our mass. Strip the atmosphere away and you have a planet that is, structurally, nearly our twin — within the goldilocks zone as well. So, I posed myself a simple question on a Monday morning: What would it take to terraform Venus?

---

## Terraforming

### Surface Temperature

The first obstacle is temperature. Venus's surface sits at around 460°C, driven by the most extreme greenhouse effect in the solar system. CO2 is extraordinarily effective at trapping heat. The rise from just 0.03% to 0.04% in Earth's atmosphere is what's warming our planet today; Venus's atmosphere is 97% CO2 at 93 times Earth's atmospheric pressure. The surface pressure would feel like being 900 meters underwater. The planet needs to be cooled, and the most direct way to do that is to cut off its supply of sunlight.

The solution is a very large mirror. Not a complex one: a massive, ultra-thin sheet of reflective aluminum, about 30 nanometers thick, positioned between Venus and the Sun. At that thickness, aluminum achieves essentially its maximum reflectivity, while the material itself weighs roughly 0.08 grams per square meter, making it extraordinarily light. Even a structure large enough to shade an entire planet stays within a manageable mass range, far less than many large-scale things humans have already built. With sunlight blocked, Venus begins radiating its accumulated heat into space.

The harder engineering problem isn't building the mirror, it's keeping it in position because solar radiation pressure (the constant force exerted by photons on any reflective surface) would push a structure that thin out of place almost immediately. The solution, first proposed by physicist Paul Birch in 1991, is to abandon the idea of a single flat disk entirely; Instead, the mirror is made up of many angled rings, arranged so that each ring reflects incoming sunlight onto the next, redirecting it step by step until it exits from the back of the structure. This cancels out the radiation pressure on the front, and the whole system holds itself in position through geometry rather than any active propulsion.

### The Great Flood

With the sun blocked, Venus begins to cool. For the first few decades, the change is slow; the atmosphere stays dense and completely hostile, and still nothing on the surface would yet survive. Around the 60-year mark (this is a rough guess), we begin to see noticeable change.

When the temperature drops to roughly 31°C, CO2 reaches a phase transition at that pressure; not from gas to solid, but from gas to liquid. At 93 times Earth's atmospheric pressure, CO2 behaves differently than it does in everyday experience, and the phase diagram of CO2 tells you exactly where this shift occurs: at high enough pressures, there is a region where CO2 exists as liquid at temperatures that would feel warm to us.

When Venus hits that region, and the atmosphere begins to rain down as liquid CO2, a global rainstorm falls across the planet (the duration of which is debatable). As it rains, atmospheric pressure and temperature begin dropping together. Eventually, at around -56°C, the pressure falls to about seven times Earth's and the liquid CO2 oceans freeze solid. At -81°C, CO2 snow covers everything. What remains in the atmosphere is mostly nitrogen, at about three times Earth's surface pressure; essentially a much much colder planet, devoid of oxygen, but far less harsh.

### Ship in an Ocean

A frozen planet without water isn't a home. We need oceans, and the closest reliable source of water at the scale we need is Europa, a moon of Jupiter. Europa is 3,120 kilometers wide, with an icy outer shell roughly 10 to 25 kilometers thick and a saltwater ocean beneath it estimated to be between 60 and 150 kilometers deep. In total, Europa contains roughly 2.3 times as much water as all of Earth's oceans combined, a very attractive 'water reservoir' in the solar system.

It'd be fun to take a page out of Despicable Me and steal a moon! But the attempt would probably be futile. We could however deploy construction drones to cut ice from Europa's surface and launch those chunks toward Venus using mass drivers — which are electromagnetic launch rails that accelerate payloads using magnetic fields, making chemical rockets unnecessary on smaller bodies. To escape Europa's gravity, the ice needs to reach about 2,026 m/s, while the interplanetary transfer trajectory to Venus requires roughly another 6,500 m/s. Solar sails attached to the ice packages can adjust the trajectory en route, allowing delivery even when Jupiter and Venus are not in ideal alignment for a minimum-energy transfer.

When the ice arrives at Venus, it's traveling between 17 and 40 kilometers per second. Dropping it directly onto the surface would reheat the planet we've spent well over a century cooling. Instead, orbital tethers catch the incoming ice and convert its kinetic energy into useful work. A magnetic nozzle on an orbiting space station transforms the high-velocity ice into plasma at the moment of impact, absorbing its momentum. That momentum transfers through the tether system to frozen CO2 on the surface below, catching it and accelerating it into orbit for removal. On average, one kilogram of incoming water ice helps lift roughly 3.17 kilograms of CO2 off Venus, meaning the water delivery and the atmospheric removal happen simultaneously, each one powering the other. After a few centuries of this, Venus is covered in shallow oceans averaging a couple hundred meters deep, with continents and islands beginning to form.

### Seeding with Life

The atmosphere at this point is mostly nitrogen with the right pressure and roughly the right temperature, but with no oxygen. You could walk outside in regular clothes with nothing more than an oxygen mask. The first inhabitants of Venus won't be human though — they'd be cyanobacteria.

Cyanobacteria are the reason Earth has oxygen. Between 2.45 and 2.22 billion years ago, they triggered what geologists call the Great Oxidation Event, transforming a toxic and anoxic atmosphere into one capable of supporting complex animal life through oxygenic photosynthesis, by splitting water molecules and releasing oxygen as a byproduct. They also fix atmospheric nitrogen by converting it into compounds that other organisms can absorb such as nitrate and nitrite; essentially, this fertilizes the ocean for the life that could follow.

On land, the process is slower. The Venusian surface is almost entirely hard volcanic rock. Before plants can take root, that rock needs to be ground down, mixed with water, and seeded with bacteria, mosses, and lichen, following the same natural succession that builds soil on freshly cooled lava flows on Earth's volcanic islands. Once a thin layer of soil forms, more complex plants can follow, and eventually forests. To accelerate the process, CO2 is strategically released from storage to boost photosynthesis, while orbital mirrors extend the effective day which keeps plants active longer than Venus's natural rotation would allow on its own (a solar day of about 116 Earth days).

As genetic engineering matures, we may be able to engineer species purpose-built for this environment; for purposes such as, higher photosynthesis efficiency, optimized nitrogen fixation, and faster oxygen output. All told, it takes several thousand years to reach a breathable atmosphere, and longer for our eventual cities and cultures.

---

## Closing Thoughts

The bottleneck here is not technical. Every step above is grounded in physics we understand, chemistry we've already done, and engineering principles we're actively developing. The mirror, the mass drivers, the tethers, the biology: none of it requires a physics breakthrough. It requires scale, time, and sustained collective will across generations. The cathedrals of medieval Europe took 200 years to build. The engineers who laid the foundations never saw the spires. The aqueducts of Rome were planned across generations by people who understood they were building for someone else entirely. They did it anyway. There's something clarifying in that. Not every problem worth solving is one you'll live to finish. Some of the most important work anyone can do is to start the right thing, do it well, and hand it forward.
