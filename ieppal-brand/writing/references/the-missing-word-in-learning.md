# The Missing Word In Learning

I recently finished listening to an episode from David Senra's Founders podcast, titled *Lessons from Jeff Bezos's Shareholder Letters*.

There was a lot of information to unpack, but what struck me was the idea that people must be cognizant of proxy versus process.

Bezos actually explained that "It is not rare to hear a junior leader defend a bad outcome with something like, 'Well, we followed the process.' A more experienced leader will use it as an opportunity to investigate and improve the process."

He continued by explaining that the ability to separate proxy versus process is one of the many examples of how Amazon stays operationally excellent — one of Bezos's core pillars. He made it clear that process must be improved through scope; In fact, he says "Unrealistic beliefs on scope… [are] often hidden and undiscussed … kill high standards."

At this point, your curious mind might think, what does organizational excellence at my deodorant provider have to do with me? Understanding the process, of your learning, is what creates a high standard of knowledge. But before we can improve the process, we must investigate. We begin with scope.

## Wrong Expectations of Scope

In three simple steps, I am able to transcribe information into my own words and make connections; yet, occasionally I will come away feeling less than confident with what I've learned. Obsidian is useful in its ability to draw these connections between ideas (notes which are unique files), but this alone is not always enough.

Fortunately for me, Bezos's learnings at Amazon — albeit in a business setting — draw a fitting parallel to how we should approach learning.

As we know, Bezos's goal is to maintain and grow operational excellence at Amazon, so senior leadership (Bezos included) mandated all meetings to be anchored to the writing a multi-page memo; however, upper-level management quickly realized the existence of such a requirement was still not enough to nurture high standards.

Bezos knew the memo was powerful, but he spent a disproportionate amount of time wondering why memo quality fluctuated so much across meetings, departments, and people — until it finally struck him…

> "When a memo isn't great, it's not the writer's inability to recognize a high standard, but instead a wrong expectation on scope. They mistakenly believe a high standard six-page memo can be written in one or two days or even a few hours when in reality it might take a week or more … The great memos are written and rewritten, shared with colleagues who are asked to improve the work, set aside for a couple of days, and then edited again with a fresh mind. They simply cannot be done in a day or two. The key point here is that you can improve results through the simple act of teaching scope. That a great memo should probably take a week or more."

## The Scope of High-Standard Learning

I realize that my learning process was not poor, it was simply unfinished. It remained unfinished because the expectation on the scope of my learning was not clearly defined.

I expected that once I transcribed my notes and made connections that the application of that knowledge would come naturally to me — without any attempt to practice. The difference between experience and expectation only grew when I failed to realize that learning — in the way I expected — could not be done in isolation. If I wanted to better understand free cash flow, I probably should've asked my mother who's a business professor.

Finally, understanding that learning is an iterative process and does not stop when a textbook chapter has been consumed back to front, is part of defining our scope. Even months after consuming a textbook chapter, the consistent application of its theory is what helps you retain knowledge — and make you feel as though you've achieved a high standard of learning.

Understand the difference between proxy and process, challenge your process, and clear define expectations on scope.

Thank you Bezos, for both the deodorant and lesson.
