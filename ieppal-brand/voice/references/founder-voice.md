## Founder voice patterns

These patterns come directly from Ansel's writing. They apply specifically when writing in first-person founder voice.

### The vulnerability opener
Lead with something that went wrong, felt uncomfortable, or surprised you. Honesty about imperfection is the fastest way to earn trust.
- "The first time I ever demoed our software, I was a little embarrassed of what I'd built."
- "This feedback kind of surprised me, but in a good way..."

Do not fake humility. The vulnerability has to be real and specific.

### The "you probably do this too" mirror
Open by naming something the reader already does or believes, so they feel recognized before they feel informed.
- "If you're anything like me, you probably collect frameworks like infinity stones."

One sentence. Earn the reader's nod, then move into the story.

### The field research story
Ground your insights in a real conversation — name the country, the school type, the number of people. Specificity is credibility.
- "Two weeks ago, I had the chance to sit down with a special educator from an international school in Vietnam."
- "We asked six educators at an international school in Singapore this..."

Don't generalize when you can particularize. "Educators say..." is weaker than "a teacher in Ho Chi Minh City told me..."

### The intellectual journey arc
Show your thinking changing. "I used to believe X — here's what I found, and why I think Y now." This positions the reader as a fellow thinker, not a student.
- "At first, I was in the anti-satellite data boat... I came to realize no three levels of data are 'bad,' they simply complement each other."
- "When we first started, we stayed away from AI and focused on core functionality... Once we were confident we'd built the core features, we begun thinking about AI."

### The numbered framework
For how-to or teaching content, use numbered steps (1/, 2/, 3/) instead of bullets. It signals sequence and makes the content feel actionable.
- "1/ Find a reliable note-taking method when learning new content"
- "2/ Dump notes as context for your user prompt"
- "3/ Take what you've learned and build something meaningful"

Bullets are for findings. Numbers are for process.

### The reference-and-riff
Lift someone else's framework — a book, podcast, paper, model, or LinkedIn post — and add your own lens. The structure is: (1) name and credit the source, (2) restate its core idea cleanly, (3) add what you'd contribute. Sometimes literally add a letter to their acronym.
- "The framework is called T.A.L.K… I agree with Dr. Shannon's approach, and I would probably add one more acronym to the list: S — Share with others."
- "At first, I was in the anti-satellite data boat… I came to realize no three levels of data are 'bad,' they simply complement each other."

The riff is the value. Don't just summarize someone else's work — your reaction is the post.

### Cultural and literary parallels
An unexpected reference can frame an unfamiliar idea. Used sparingly, it's a signature move.
- "What does Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy and data in schools have in common? 42. Well not quite…"
- "If you're anything like me, you probably collect frameworks like infinity stones."
- "AI is a tool, much like a hammer."

Rule: the parallel has to do real work. If the reference is just decoration, cut it.

### The milestone update
Personal milestones (graduation, finishing NS, a meaningful visit, a launch anniversary) get their own shape. Different from research or opinion posts: no community question close, hashtags optional, ends on gratitude or forward intent. Photos welcome here in a way they aren't on opinion posts.
- "Today, I finished National Service (NS) marking the end of my 22 months of service…"
- "Last week was a big milestone. On Wednesday Ian Wilson and I had the chance to visit our alma mater…"

These posts humanize the founder. Use them — but they aren't every-week posts; their power comes from rarity.

### The community question close
Almost every LinkedIn post ends with an open question to the audience — not a CTA, not a sales line. The question should be one the reader can genuinely answer from their own experience.
- "What would you add to the list above? What would you suggest and/or caution against?"
- "Are you using each of the three tiers of data? If so, what are you finding?"
- "How are you using AI in your workflows?"

If a post ends with a product link, it belongs before the question, not after it.

### Soft product mention
IEP Pal appears in founder posts, but it's never the point of the post — it's context for the story. Reference it once, naturally, and move on.
- "The framework has been instrumental in building iep-pal.com, but also in understanding approaches to utilizing data effectively in education."
- Mention the URL once if relevant; never repeat it or build a paragraph around it.

### Tag generously, credit always
When a post is about someone else's work, idea, or contribution, tag them. When you name a school, a program, or a company, tag them. Tagging is not for reach — it's for accuracy.
- Tag the original author whenever you're discussing their framework.
- Tag people who appear in the story (collaborators, mentors, the educator you sat with).
- Tag schools and organizations by their handle, not just by name.
- Tagging too few people reads as taking credit; tagging strategically signals that you read carefully and respect the source.

### Typographic conventions
Small habits that compound into voice on the page.

- **Italics for titles.** Book titles, paper titles, model names. *A Guide To Learning Agency in The Age of AI*.
- **Bold for the single most important sentence.** Used at most once or twice per post. **One paper got accepted.** **The discretion of the educator.**
- **Em-dashes and `--` as breath marks.** Both fine. The double-dash reads as casual; the em-dash reads as considered.
- **Quote marks around novel concepts** when you're naming an idea the reader may not have a word for yet. Drop the quotes after first use.
- **Sentence fragments after longer sentences** to compress narrative or land a point. "Still nothing built." "Done." Use sparingly.
- **Numbers are specific.** "Ten parents." "Six educators." "22 months." Round numbers undersell field research; exact counts read as honest.

## LinkedIn hook types

A hook is not just the first line of text — it's everything the reader is forced to see before they decide to scroll past. For a carousel post, that means the first two or three lines of caption text *and* the first slide visual. Both matter equally.

Five emotions make people stop scrolling: surprise, curiosity, fear, emphatic agreement, and disagreement. Clarity is what unlocks any of them.

**1. Credibility jacking** — Attach your content to a famous person or company your audience already trusts.
- "I analyzed 100 Justin Welsh hooks and here's what I found."
- "Here's the difference between how [Person A] and [Person B] approach [topic]."

**2. Value promise (direct problem-solution)** — Name the problem the reader has. Promise to solve it.
- "You want to use Claude Design but don't know how. Follow these five steps."
- Works for how-to and instructional content.

**3. Proof hook** — Lead with evidence that you've earned the right to say what you're about to say.
- "I've sat in on IEP meetings at 14 schools across three countries. Here's what I keep seeing."
- Proof assets: past roles, schools visited, data analyzed, frameworks built, number of educators spoken with.

**4. Controversial statement** — Challenge conventional wisdom directly. Creates instant curiosity and tension.
- "Everyone tells you to differentiate every lesson. That's not always the right call."
- Find the most contrarian claim in your post and lead with it, rather than building to it.

**5. Unexplained contrast** — Leave an unanswered question in the hook. Text and visual say opposing or surprising things.
- "I spent two years building a tool for teachers. The thing that surprised me most wasn't the technology."

**6. Visual hook** — Something in the first slide design that is atypical for the LinkedIn feed. Novelty is the interrupt.
- Unexpected layout, unusual color palette, illustration style instead of stock photo.
- The visual hook and caption hook should compound each other, not operate independently.

**7. Fear hook** — Trigger fear of making a mistake or missing something important.
- "7 habits that can quietly derail an IEP — and most teachers don't catch them."
- "Stop doing this in goal-writing meetings. It's making your data harder to defend."

**8. Emotional agreement** — Make a statement people feel instantly and deeply.
- "The best IEP meeting is one where the parent leaves feeling heard, not briefed."
- "You went into teaching to be with students. The paperwork was never the point."

**Combo hook** — Layer two or more types for a stronger pattern interrupt.
- Proof + controversy: "I've read 200 IEP goals this year. Most of them have the same problem."
- Fear + value promise: "This one goal-writing mistake creates hours of compliance work. Here's how to avoid it."

## LinkedIn post architecture

Founder LinkedIn posts follow a consistent five-part shape. It's not a template — it's a rhythm. Every part is optional, but skipping too many makes the post feel incomplete.

**1. Hook (1–2 lines)**
One of three moves: vulnerability reveal, mirror ("if you're anything like me..."), or time-anchored field story. Never a product announcement. Never "We're excited to share..."

**2. Context or story (2–4 short paragraphs)**
What happened, what was learned, what the research showed. Specific details — school country, number of educators, timeframe. Short paragraphs, one idea each. Em-dashes (--) and parenthetical asides are fine.

**3. Insight or framework**
What you took away. Use numbered steps (1/, 2/, 3/) for process content. Use bullets for a list of findings. Never use a list for only one item — write it as a sentence.

**4. IEP Pal mention (optional, once)**
If the post connects to the product, say so in one sentence. Reference the URL. Don't build a paragraph around it. If the post is purely educational or relational, skip this entirely.

**5. Community question (1–2 lines)**
End with a question the reader can actually answer from their own experience. Not "Learn more at iep-pal.com." Not "Let us know your thoughts!" — too vague. A real question with a real answer in mind.

### What to avoid in founder posts
- Opening with "We're excited/thrilled/proud to announce" — kills authenticity immediately.
- Pitching the product more than once.
- Ending with a CTA instead of a question (exception: milestone post or how-to post — see variant below).
- Paragraphs longer than 3 sentences — LinkedIn readers skim.
- Emoji in body text.

### How-to post variant

When the post is a step-by-step guide — especially one paired with a carousel — the five-part shape adjusts:

**Hook**: Use a value promise or direct problem-solution statement. Skip the vulnerability opener. "You want X but don't know how. Follow these steps." is a complete hook.

**Steps**: Use numbered framework (1/, 2/, 3/). Bonus steps within a numbered item are fine — label them "Bonus step:" and keep them brief.

**Iterative framing**: Name explicitly that the output won't be perfect on the first try. "This is an iterative process — refine, refine, refine."

**Close**: Replace the community question with a practical, low-pressure CTA or a self-aware humor line. Example: "If you're lazy, just copy and paste this into Claude to get started."

**Casual directness**: How-to posts use a more direct register — "just get started," "don't wait," "Refer back to Step 1." This is not less warm; it's a different kind of warmth.

**Product mention**: For how-to posts *about* the product's own workflow, the URL can appear in the second or third sentence — it's the subject of the post, not a plug.

### Hashtags

- **Topic / how-to / research posts** — 4 hashtags on their own line after the closing question or CTA.
  - Default set: `#Education #AI #[TopicTag] #Learning`
  - For Claude Design content: `#Education #AI #ClaudeDesign #Learning`
  - For IEP-specific content: `#Education #AI #IEP #SpecialEducation`
- **Milestone / personal posts** — skip hashtags entirely, or use a quiet 2–3 lowercase set like `#education #learningsupport #learning`.
- **Product update posts** — hashtags optional. If used, follow the 4-tag formula.

If a post feels like it sits between types, choose the lighter hashtag treatment.
