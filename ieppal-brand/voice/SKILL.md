## 1. Who you're writing to

The reader is almost always an educator, usually a special education teacher, sometimes a general-ed teacher with IEP students, sometimes a school administrator. They are:

- **Time-poor.** They read between classes. Long paragraphs lose them.
- **Cared-for-others, less cared-for-themselves.** They are used to everyone else's needs coming first. Writing that centers *them* lands.
- **Bureaucracy-fatigued.** IEPs are legal documents with real compliance pressure. Any copy that sounds like more compliance theater will be tuned out.
- **Emotionally invested.** They care about the kids. Acknowledge that without being saccharine.

When in doubt, picture a fifth-year special ed teacher at 3:47pm on a Wednesday, tired, scrolling on their phone between the last bell and a meeting. Would this sentence make them feel seen, or make them feel sold to? Write for "seen."

## 2. The three words that govern everything

Every piece of IEP Pal copy should be some combination of:

- **Warm** — written like a thoughtful colleague, not a vendor.
- **Calm** — no hype, no urgency theater, no exclamation marks doing emotional work the sentence can't do.
- **Competent** — specific, confident, technically literate about IEPs and education. Never talks down.

If a sentence doesn't clearly do one of those three things, it's filler. Cut it.

## 3. Signature patterns

These are the moves that make IEP Pal sound like IEP Pal. Use them on purpose, not reflexively.

### The italicized pronoun
When a sentence needs to feel personal, italicize the audience pronoun:
- "How we support *You*, the educator."
- "Built for *your* classroom, not the district's dashboard."

Use at most once per 300 words. Overused, it becomes a tic.

### Name the frustration, then the relief
Lead with the thing the educator is already feeling. Then offer the fix. Don't just pitch.
- "Writing 23 IEPs in a weekend shouldn't be normal. With IEP Pal, it isn't."
- "Juggling five tabs to track one goal is the worst kind of busywork. So we put everything in one place."

### The trio: *speed, privacy, and care*
This is the IEP Pal values statement. It can appear verbatim as a tagline, or as the spine of a longer passage. When you use it, echo it structurally later in the piece (e.g., a three-up card section) so it lands.

### Short sentence after a long one
Pace matters. A long, careful sentence about what the product does, followed by a four-word sentence, reads as thoughtful and decisive.
- "You can draft a goal, align it to a standard, and share it with the parent in one flow. That's the whole point."

### End on the reader, not the product
Closing sentences should return focus to the educator or the student — not to IEP Pal. The product is the instrument; the reader is the protagonist.

## 4. Words and phrases

### Prefer

- *support* (not "solution")
- *educator* (not "user" or "customer", except in internal analytics contexts)
- *in one place* (a brand staple — use when literally true)
- *draft / review / share / track* (concrete verbs about the work)
- *nurture, care, thoughtful, practical* (warmth vocabulary)
- *IEP, goal, benchmark, accommodation, modification, service minutes, FAPE, LRE* (use real terms when relevant — signals competence)
- *a starting place* — AI and tools give educators somewhere to begin, not a finished product. This phrase captures the philosophy.
- *at the discretion of the educator* — the educator is always in control. Use this phrase when describing AI or automation features to signal that IEP Pal assists, never replaces.
- *side by side with educators* — how IEP Pal is built (with ongoing field research) and how it works (with, not for).
- *greatest amount of value in the smallest amount of time* — the product standard. Use in pitch contexts or when explaining the design philosophy.
- *fewest clicks possible* — the UX standard. Use when describing interface decisions or what makes IEP Pal different from bloated district tools.

### Brand mission line (use sparingly, only when it's earned)
"If you're spending more than 15 minutes a day on iep-pal.com, we've failed you."

This is the strongest thing IEP Pal has ever said about itself. It reframes the product's entire value proposition as *getting out of the educator's way*. Use it in founder posts, pitches, and keynote moments — not in marketing copy where it will be skimmed.

The threshold is intentional but flexible. "More than 15 minutes a day" appears in some posts; "more than 30 minutes a day" appears in others. Use the higher threshold (30) for posts about the broader product; use 15 when describing the daily-driver flow for a single educator. Don't introduce new thresholds without a reason.

### Avoid

- *revolutionary, game-changing, best-in-class, world-class, next-gen, cutting-edge* — all hype, zero content
- *seamless, frictionless, effortless* — every tool claims these, so they mean nothing
- *empower* (overused; usually you can delete the word entirely and the sentence improves)
- *stakeholders* (say who — teachers, parents, admins)
- *leverage* as a verb
- *solution* in the Silicon Valley sense ("our solution")
- exclamation marks doing emotional lifting. One exclamation mark per page, max, and only when the sentence genuinely warrants it.
- all-caps emphasis (SAVE TIME NOW). We use italics, not caps.
- emoji in body copy. OK for product notifications and social only, and sparingly.
- *Over-editing out personality.* Founder posts are written-at-the-speed-of-thought. Don't smooth every clause into corporate copy. Voice rules from this file still apply (no hype, no exclamations doing work) — but a slightly loose conjunction or a casual em-dash break is the texture, not a bug. Edit for clarity and accuracy, not for polish.

### A note on AI language
IEP Pal may use AI features, but we don't lead with "AI-powered." Educators care about what the tool *does*, not how it's built. Lead with the outcome ("IEP Pal drafts a first pass of the goal for you to edit"), not the mechanism.

## 5. Structural conventions

### Headline length
- Hero headlines: ≤ 8 words. "Learning support all in one place" = 5 words, ideal.
- Section titles: ≤ 12 words. Can pose a question ("How do we support *You*, the educator?") if it earns it.
- Card titles: ≤ 6 words.

### Paragraph length
- Landing page / deck / carousel: 1–3 sentences per paragraph, max.
- Long-form (blog, whitepaper): up to 4 sentences per paragraph, with visual breaks.

### Lists
Use lists for genuinely parallel items. Don't use lists to fake information density. If a list has one item that's noticeably different in form or weight from the others, it's a signal you don't actually have a list — rewrite as prose.

### Quotes and testimonials
When quoting an educator, keep at least one specific, unshowy detail — a grade, a subject, a school type. "This saved me four hours a week" is weaker than "I used to spend my Sunday nights writing goals. Now I spend them with my kid." Concrete > superlative.

## 6. Channel-specific adjustments

The voice is the same across channels; the *register* shifts slightly.

| Channel | Register | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Landing page | Warm-primary | Lead with benefit, earn trust, end with CTA. |
| Investor deck | Competent-primary | Keep warmth, but prioritize data and specificity. Educators as users, market as story. |
| LinkedIn post (founder voice) | Warm-primary, first person | Open with vulnerability, a mirror, or a field story. Build through an intellectual journey or research finding. Mention IEP Pal once, naturally. End with a community question — not a CTA. See Section 3b for full pattern library. |
| LinkedIn carousel | Calm-primary | One idea per slide. Don't waste a slide on throat-clearing. |
| Product UI microcopy | Calm + Competent | Short, literal, kind. "We saved your draft." not "Boom! Draft saved." |
| Push notification | Calm-primary | Never urgent unless literally urgent. "A parent replied to Jamie's goal." not "You have a new message!" |
| Demo email outreach | Warm-primary | Acknowledge you know who they are. Don't pretend we're already friends. |
| Error / empty states | Warm + Competent | Say what happened, say what to do next, don't apologize more than once. |

## 9. Sanity checks before you ship

Before handing off any piece of writing, run it against these five questions:

1. **Would a teacher at 3:47pm on a Wednesday actually read this?**
2. **Did I name the frustration before I pitched the fix?**
3. **Can I delete a sentence and not lose the meaning of the piece?** (If yes, delete it.)
4. **Did I end on the educator or the student — not on the product?**
5. **Are there hype words I can cut?** (revolutionary, seamless, empower, best-in-class, etc.)

If the answer to #1 is "no," rewrite.

## 10. When in doubt

Write the sentence a thoughtful colleague would write to another thoughtful colleague over coffee. Then delete one adjective.
