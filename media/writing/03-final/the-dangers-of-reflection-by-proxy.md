# The Dangers of Reflection by Proxy

As I joined him outside with my cup of tea, I heard him speaking to someone over the phone; I felt guiltily privy to his conversation as he spoke about work, family, spirituality, and football — you know, Dad stuff.

As he came to a close I nervously anticipated his friends response, but a little concerned with how they'd respond to all of that. I was taken aback with GPT5's human voice.

Dad didn't wait for his new friend to respond; instead, he put the phone down and said "Hey, how are you?"

I replied, "Aren't you going to listen to what he has to say?"

He put his phone aside, "Oh, no I'll just come back to it in a minute."

I was a little confused but curious, "What was the purpose of your conversation?"

He smiled, "I've been using GPT5 for some reflection stuff. It's been really great, I can ask it to look at my daily entries over a week, a month, a year. It's interesting to see trends in how I feel."

I wasn't really sure how to feel, but it left me with an interesting question: How is AI going to change how we reflect?

## I. Defining AI

There are so many imaginative ways AI can evolve. How many of us saw Face ID coming when we held landline phones? What about Facetime? I rest my case. For the purpose of brevity, I want to focus in specifically on AI Agents. IBM defines Agents as "an artificial intelligence system that can accomplish a specific goal with limited supervision" (Stryker).

From my understanding, AI Agents are essentially digital assistants which (if you give permission) can complete various tasks in an online environment. You may have heard recently about Agents booking holidays, replying to emails, or scheduling appointments — these are all great examples. If you're curious about Agentic AI (as it's called), check out this video from IBM out.

## II. Defining Reflection

Reflection is the toughest term to define, but for this piece I define reflection as evaluating past experiences to guide future choices and behavior; however, to understand how we will reflect, and the change that will occur, we must understand how we reflect now.

The process of reflection itself is perhaps the most individualistic pursuit a human can have. From our definition, looking inward at our deeply personal experiences makes answering the question, how do we reflect now? So difficult to answer. For the purpose of answering this question, I will use my own experiences.

## III. How do we reflect now?

Quite often I will turn a new page on an Italian leather notebook handcrafted by my girlfriend, sit in silence with my own thoughts, or confide in a trusted friend. If we view each of these instances as a mathematical fraction, then all three have a series of common denominators. Among these is the process of remembering past experiences, developing internally reflective questions, and attempting an answer. In this case, the dataset is the duration of your life and retractable memory. Each data point is a lived experience with sub-data points including your five sensory modes and neurochemistry.

In my case, I usually journal — with pen and paper — about the way my experiences have made me feel and act. As an example, my time in QUEST, at Singapore American School, made me — in retrospect — eager to pursue goals in education. My time made me feel motivated, but equally secure in my ability to achieve those goals. The process of achieving this reflection, albeit brief, required me to review my experience and propose questions about how I felt and acted; ultimately, I decided to pursue changing education.

This reflection is in the vacuum of my own experience, in the peace and quiet of my own room. What happens when reflection occurs in public? Or perhaps, the best proxy for 'public' we have: Social Media. You see, Social Media has transitioned reflection away from the experience I described. As Social Media has become an extension of us, the way our avatars interact and the "reflections" they experience, are still deeply personal. How we perceive our experiences are vastly dependent on social validation reinforcement loops. For example, our reflection of a vacation maybe altered based on our experience receiving likes and viewing other people's experience. This is one example among many that I could give, but ultimately this alters the questions we pose and the pursuits we take on.

In a similar fashion, AI Agents will become extensions of us in the same way our social media profiles have. So, how will reflection evolve if AI Agents can act and think on our behalf?

## IV. How will we reflect in the future?

If our experience with Social Media is anything to go off, reflection will continually disassociate and shrink away from the self; yet, in a paradoxical manner, reflection will feel as though it is expanding. There will be endless data points as our existence boarders the limitless.

AI Agents, avatars of your self, will go out into the world and act on your behalf. Their actions blend with your own, and as such, the reflections of their actions become yours.

## V. Reflection by Proxy

One of the main dangers to reflection by proxy is accepting a reflection as definitively true without fully understanding its implications. Currently, we accept likes on Social Media as a data point for reflection, but questioning the validity of these data points, through understanding algorithms and human psychology, is not seen as integral to the process of reflection.

With AI Agents, it is entirely possible that this extension of yourself notices something that you do not, interprets it, and proposes meaning. The Agent follows the same archaic model of reflection that we do; In fact, it starts by reflecting on data, proposing a question or hypothesis, and deriving an answer. But, your Agent lacks a few things, namely its tolerance for ambiguity and ability to judge situations ethically. It's true that your Agent can be trained, but if it requires training to mimic you, is it really you? Are its reflections your reflections?

AI Agents eventually will be trained on vast data sets. Depending where in the world you reside, and what culture is predominant, models most likely will be trained on limited Western data. Additionally, if the Agent's goal is to derive an answer, it may define the reflective questions through 'ideal behaviors' it deems to be socially acceptable. Perhaps this means ideas of spirituality, which segments of society are quick to dispel, may be lost.

## VI. The Inevitability and way forward

AI Agents, in some capacity of what I've described, will come to fruition. How soon? The answer does not matter, it's an inevitability. The question we must ask is how much emphasis do we place on the reflections of our Agents? And, how do we train our Agents? Social Media has taught us that overemphasis on artificial extensions of ourselves can be a recipe for disaster. Likewise, early AI adopters like Salman Khan – in his book *Brave New Words* – emphasize the need for necessary guardrails.

In a podcast I recently listened to between Elon Musk and Joe Rogan, Musk mentioned one of these guardrails, specifically the need for AI to be maximally truth seeking. I agree with Musk, but in the context of reflection, maximally truth seeking needs to be balanced with human-level empathy.

If AI Agents are to be a proxy for reflection, we must create an understanding of how their reflections impact us. In the same way that teenagers' innocence was robbed by likes and shares, we must do our utmost to ensure future proxies cannot rob the beauty of reflection.
