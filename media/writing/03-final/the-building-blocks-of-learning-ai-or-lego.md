# The Building Blocks of Learning. AI or LEGO?

From early 2010 to late 2014, I was placed in charge of an incredibly large infrastructure project here in Singapore. Any ideas? Correct, the LEGO city I started building when I was four.

Luckily for me, growing up only a few years after the iPhone meant that almost all of my free time as a kid was spent playing with both LEGO and other kids in the same apartment complex.

Some of my most memorable achievements with play came from the creativity I had; In fact, two moments stand out now in retrospect. Every couple of Sunday's my dad would reluctantly agree to help me build something from my imagination. The reluctance on his part came from the fact I wasn't very much help, but he's always underestimated my words of encouragement. One Sunday, we built a very DIY submarine, I was absolutely thrilled. It looked exactly like the one I'd seen on Discovery Channel the night before.

Fast forward a few months, I wanted to take my creations to the air. I wanted to build a LEGO plane. The problem? I didn't have a ton of niche Lego bricks. My solution? Take six 2x4 bricks and build the simplest plane imaginable. It worked.

*[Image: Lego plane design using six 2x4 bricks]*

What those Sunday's taught me was that, what I imagined was possible to build was buildable. The creations were not always perfect, but that was not the point; play taught me that if I could come up with an idea, handle the problems that arose, I could build it totally from scratch. Sometimes it took trading parts with a friend, the engineering genius of my father, and my unfaltering desire to make my vision a reality; yet, it was always LEGO that shaped my journey as a learner.

Fast forward to the present day, learning and play seem to be mentioned in the same breath as AI, given the latter's relevance. I didn't realize at the time, but I'm starting to think that LEGO was AI's cooler older sibling.

From what I'd read on LinkedIn, Medium, and other social sites, I began to form the belief that AI could take over teaching subject matter to students. I believed that teachers would shift to a more coach-like role, but I think I made a fatal mistake with this train of thought. I worry we're mistaking efficiency for learning.

Khan Academy recently rolled out several AI functions, including Khanmigo. While I trust Sal Khan and his team to build an AI with the necessary guardrails for education, what is the extent it can help us really learn? Take this as an example. You're a teacher who has given full autonomy to your student to build their own LEGO city. Your goal as the teacher is to nurture the group to collectively agree to a set rules and responsibilities. Your concern is less with how the city expands or grows, and more focused on creativity, compromise, and empathy. In my opinion,

> AI can do many things, but it cannot substitute play.

LEGO, and the play that came with, taught me fundamental lessons that shaped me as a human. I had to be creative when management (My parents) wouldn't allocate me greater budget for more materials (more bricks). I had to problem solve when my limited resources meant I was constrained to a certain type of building style. These limitations forced me negotiate with friends for pieces, sometimes returning favors long after. I became far more empathetic from the mutual generosity I experienced as a child, than sitting in front of an Ipad watching someone else experience it instead. People don't want to hear it because it isn't fashionable or good for business, but play is life's most advanced learning technology. LEGO just happens to be a vehicle for that.

You know, I'm often drawn back to an article I read a while ago, written by Damian Bacchoo, titled *Why Schools Must Hire More Rebels*. Damian describes rebels beautifully, explaining that "they challenge the status quo of the industrial model of school education, experiment with pedagogy, ask hard questions, and challenge rigid frameworks and conformity." If you haven't read the full article, I highly recommend it. What relevance does this have to play? With the increasing push from schools to adapt AI and ensure students are technologically literate, the need for rebels is abundantly clear.

We need rebels who still believe in play. We need rebels who believe in imagination, collaboration, problem-solving, and empathy.

Remember, Luke Skywalker became a rebel because he had Obi-Wan Kenobi, a Jedi Master guiding him. We have a generation of Luke Skywalkers, its our decision on how we teach them to use a lightsaber.
