# Schools Must Double Down On Prompting. Or wait, was it prompting?

I recently came across a repost by a former teacher of mine, Dinesh Ayyappan, on LinkedIn. The hook of the post read

> "AI agents have entered the conversation about institutions, and we need to be really judicious here."

Dinesh went on to argue,

> "What is an institution when you can just delete, transmute, or substitute its agents? And what might we lose when we design institutions around, or for, AI agents as actors or participants?"

That last question stuck with me, putting me in déjà vu trance – reminding me of a pervious idea I'd written about, titled *The Dangers of Reflection by Proxy*. I attempted to answer that question through focusing on the function of reflection. Specifically, I argued from the perspective of reflection as an individualistic pursuit in battle with the "delicious data," Dinesh mentions many LLMs attempt to strip from us.

Dinesh leaves off the post with

> "I know this text may be exciting to researchers and businesses, but let's be careful here and remember that all of this is for our neighbors and families."

What I love about this last line is it humanizes the use of AI without anthropomorphizing its existence (an entirely different discussion).

Dinesh's post drew me back to a piece of writing Nick Alchin wrote titled *Do as are you are told!* Which is to say 'do not just do as you are you!' Without giving away much – because I highly recommend you read it for yourself – Nick breaks down the difference between Compliance and compliance. It is a brilliant piece and I've read the piece several times over as I contemplate the future of education. Much of that contemplation comes from my feed on LinkedIn where I see educators, and school leaders, working furiously to build frameworks around AI's use in learning; In fact, just this week we've seen International Baccalaureate (IB) share their design principles toward utilizing AI. It's all very fascinating and exciting, and yet I still feel that gaping hole of ambiguity toward the future.

I've spent weeks, almost months now, reflecting back on my journey as a learner through formal education, the military, and now university and business. I want to make the argument that there is a common thread to why my education was successful in preparing me for an uncertain world. Surprise! It was not the fancy tools we used -- though they were extremely valuable -- it was the human element. I believe that our tools shape us, and eventually we shape our tools, but in this tussle we must double down on prompting not Prompting.

## I. Prompting

Prompting, as I define it, is the process by which an individual interacts with an AI tool. The most popular AI tools are LLMs with chatbots where teachers type their prompt and get an output. These are growing in popularity across all domains, including education with examples such as MagicSchool's AI and Google Gemini seeking to develop their use cases.

Prompting is not necessarily an evil, it is a tool; however, just as a hammer is a tool, it can be used for evil. Thus, this is the typical starting point for conversations relating to AI – questions we can broadly categorize as concerns for both students and institutional approaches to learning. Prompting itself is perhaps not always explicitly mentioned, but its existence as a derivate to AI models makes it a key variable. Tied in with model hallucination, probabilistic output, and a lack of teacher involvement in system prompts, Prompting can be dangerous. Even then, I do want to note that there is tremendous work being done to understand AI. I've seen dozens of champion educators making their learnings publicly available on LinkedIn and in intimate, yet growing learning communities. This work must not cease, and a greater technical understanding may have to take precedence.

This new precedence is what I describe as the step where we shape our tools. The inevitability of AI has already begun shaping us, perhaps in the expanding positive attitudes toward inquiry and problem-based learning--through maybe we were already on the way there. At this stage, we must shape our tools through the Prompts we use. I'm a big fan of the work Chris Smith and his colleagues do at Data In Schools, and I believe the message of what Chris shares is not just applicable to data but to AI too; namely, we must understand WHY we're using AI (We shouldn't use it for the sake of using it) and structure the process appropriately from there. In this sense, AI output is data. Chris's teachings hold true.

Below you'll find the SOAR analytics model, which is an acronym for each of the steps. This is very basic model you'll learn doing a business degree and learning basic statistics, but it's a great starting place. While this may not be a perfect one-to-one application for AI, you can see the similarities between the processes.

- **Specifying the question** is understanding what we want to prompt our AI tool for.
- **Obtain the data** is supporting our prompt with context. Share files and insights (Taking privacy into account).
- **Analyze the data** is the step of discernment, perhaps the most important step. How valid are the results I'm getting?
- **Report the results.** This can look like many things, sharing results with PLCs or stakeholders such as parents and students.

*[Image: SOAR Analytics Model]*

When you lay it out it seems so extraordinarily simple! But we both know it isn't. Each step requires training and -- most importantly -- hands on practice, the type of experimentation that can be difficult in day-to-day in education. Even with frameworks, change will take time and perhaps the messiness will cause unforeseen consequences. Prompting is something we must acknowledge as a tool and move forward accordingly, together.

## II. prompting

I once sat in Kyle Aldous' beautiful Singapore American School office, zoning out at a perfectly color-coded bookshelf that satiated my OCD's internal need for control. As I sat there, feeling quite emotional from our conversation, I asked Kyle a question "How come you're always there for me when I need you? You always make time for me no matter what." Kyle smiled and explained that he had been the beneficiary of several mentors throughout his life, and beyond seeing the transactional value of it all, he wanted his mentees to be able to confide in a friend. To feel seen and feel heard. Kyle if you're reading this, thank you.

As I sit here now, my understanding is that educators tend to end up in education because of a deep internal desire to shape the lives and futures of our children. Their desire is driven by, much of what Kyle described as, wanting to be confidant for learning, failure, tears, triumph, and the messiness of watching a human shape their destiny. Forgive me if I come across as a missionary for education, but missionaries feel privileged to pay forward all they've been selflessly gifted. So while the precedence for a more technical understanding with AI tools may be unavoidable, this is where we must revisit prompting.

I define prompting as the questions we ask students on their learning journey. The definition is purposefully broad. The definition spans mediums such as inquiry-based learning, elements of design-thinking, problem-based learning, coaching, and more. In these 'non-traditional' approaches to learning, students are prompted to explore and build mental models to approach various goals and challenges. From my experience, teachers spend decades, across weekends and holidays, to learn skills beyond their domain expertise. I saw it at CoachUP8 when dozens of teachers showed up to learn about and share their passion for coaching. Hell! Even I as a student, took the time to facilitate a workshop on three coaching strategies that could be used the following Monday.

One of the things I learned from that day at Dulwich College was from Winston Coe, specifically with the OARS framework for using opened ended questions; for example, asking "how might you go about it to succeed?" (an ability question) or "how important is it for you to make this change?" (A need question). The rest of the acronym includes affirmations, reflections, and summary. As an example these look like: "Sounds like you've really though about this," "What I hearing you saying is...," and identify the most important things and feedback to summarize. It was this kind of prompting -- one that I had directly experienced as a student -- that made me appreciate my educators, my education, and the opportunities that were then opened up to me. Take a moment to think about what I just said. It was prompting that made me feel connected to other human beings, feel completely understood and secure.

The difference between Prompting and prompting, is one is a tool and the other is a way in which we make our children believe they can change the world.

## III. Prompting must not replace prompting.

Many institutions will inevitably onboard AI tools, and eventually agentic AI – probably in use cases we cannot yet foresee. Dinesh reminds us that we must remember these tools are for our neighbors, or in this case, our children. Our children.

Prompting will not replace prompting, because as long as we are raising human children, human teachers will always have a better tactile sense of learners. AI might be able to teach domain subject knowledge, but it cannot hug and console a child the same way your biggest cheerleader does.

We must not forget that social media was sold to us as a tool, a tool to connect. We look back and laugh. It is clear that promises cannot be taken at face value, and we must not believe that a Prompt can replace a prompt. A Prompt can be part of a larger ecosystem, in the same way a phone call allows us to plan that dinner party to mark a milestone. A Prompt cannot be Tik Tok, Instagram, or Facebook which suck us in with promises irreparably tainted with alterier motivates.

The difference between Prompting and prompting, is one is a tool and the other is a way in which we make our children believe they can change the world.
