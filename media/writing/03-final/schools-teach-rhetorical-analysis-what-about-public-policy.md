# Schools Teach Rhetorical Analysis, What About Public Policy?

Recently, I listened to a Dwarkesh Podcast conversation with Victor Shih. The podcast covered topics from AGI to the debt crisis in China. While Dwarkesh and Victor discussed, I couldn't help but question what I knew about policy. If someone showed me a policy brief, would I understand it? School had taught me to analyze rhetoric, but did I know how to analyze policy? The type of policy that shapes our world. I knew I needed an answer.

Doing some reflection, I recognised that in High School, rhetorical analysis skills were at the center of many classes. Starting in my freshman year (Grade 9) English course, all the way until I completed AP Language & Composition, being able to analyze rhetoric — across any medium — was an essential skill. Broadly speaking, this is what my journey with rhetoric in High School looked like:

- In grade 9, we focused on the broad rhetoric relating to the Warring States Period in China and the unification of China under Emperor Shi Huangdi.
- In grade 10, we focused on analyzing themes in *The Lord of the Flies* as well as Shakespeare's sonnets.
- In grade 11, we focused on public rhetoric surrounding the redevelopment of a rainforest, here in Singapore, into public housing. (Remember this!)
- In grade 12, we analyzed speeches from MLK, Lee Kuan Yew, and others. Reviewed modern-day rhetoric with Black Lives Matter and more.

Yet despite years of honing my rhetoric skills, my journey with rhetoric began a few years before, before the COVID pandemic. On a brisk morning in March of 2019, my dad and I found ourselves at the center of the largest demonstration in UK history: Bollocks to Brexit.

*[Image: March 23, 2019. Dad and I joining the demonstration.]*

My father is not highly opinionated, nor a politically active man, yet much of the rhetoric at home was against Britain's exit from the EU. I had seen news headlines and some YouTube videos, but I took much of what my father said at face value — I had many reasons to trust him; he is, of course, my father.

Fast forward a few years, and I found myself giving a presentation on whether Singapore's government should redevelop Clementi's rainforest for additional housing. On the surface, the redevelopment of the rainforest seemed naive. Singapore was crying out for the preservation of wildlife spaces, and if global rhetoric was anything to go off of, the environment needed more heroic stewards. So, how did I approach the issue?

1. First, I looked at data that concerned the growing need for housing in Singapore.
2. Second, I looked at data concerning species richness and preservation.
3. Third, I looked at the implications of either decision based on previous redevelopment initiatives.
4. Finally, I gave my opinion on what the government should consider.

I had just unknowingly created my first-ever draft of a policy proposal.

Reflecting now, I realize that I had the necessary skills to analyze rhetoric. If you asked me to read a column on Brexit from 2019, I could give you a fairly objective analysis; however, if you asked me what the implications of Brexit policies are, or if you asked me for policy alternatives, I would struggle to answer. So why is it that School taught me to analyze rhetoric, but not the policy that shapes the world? I guess the simple answer is: School can't teach you everything.

But even then, I owe myself a responsibility to understand both the implications of people's words and the policies that govern the societies those same people are a part of.

## So, how do you analyze policy?

For a start, we can reverse engineer frameworks used to write policy proposals and create a checklist to analyse written policies. For example, a popular framework is Bardach's Eightfold Path (Winton, 2018). This framework is named after the Buddhist Eightfold Path and is as follows:

1. Define the problem
2. Assemble the evidence
3. Construct the alternatives
4. Select the criteria
5. Project the outcomes
6. Confront the trade-offs
7. Decide
8. Tell your story

Bardach also explains that policy should pass the "taxi driver test," meaning that policy should be simplified to a point where its premise should be easily explainable in a trip — this assumes the driver does not have expert-level domain knowledge. In addition to Bardach's Eightfold Path, other frameworks include domain-specific analysis tools; for example, CAPPA (Comprehensive Analysis of Policy on Physical Activity) (Klepac Pogrmilovic et al., 2019).

Unfortunately for us, most policy is not communicated in a way that passes the taxi driver test; however, Bardach's framework (among others) gives us a reasonable gauge of what is important. Namely, we should look at: What the problem is, what stakeholders it concerns, policy content/process, and outcomes. Give it more depth and put it in a nice list, and you get this:

- **Introduction/Problem** — What problem does it claim to address? What is the framing of the problem?
- **Stakeholder & Actor Mapping** — Key actors: who are they, what are their interests, capacities, power relations? Who is left out or underrepresented?
- **Context & Constraints** — Institutional/legal/financial/political context? Historical precedents, and cultural/social norms?
- **Policy Content & Alternatives** — What does the policy state (goals; actions; resources; timeline)? What alternatives were considered or should be considered?
- **Implementation Process & Outcome Evaluation** — How will this policy be developed and implemented? How feasible is this? What mechanisms, enforcement, and actors' responsibilities are at play? What are likely or documented outcomes? Who benefits/loses? Are there evidence/data from similar policies that help project outcomes? What monitoring, feedback, and adaptability procedures are in place? Which trade-offs are acknowledged and which are not?
- **Values & Ethics** — What values are embedded or implicit (equality, efficiency, security, etc)? How do these values align with societal values? What ethical trade-offs (rights, burdens, transparency) exist, if any? How is the policy "sold" (language, metaphors, emphasis, priorities)? (Think Taxi Driver Test!)

The list seems long and scary, but if you practice using it in your analysis, you can begin to compartmentalize many of the steps. Once you have done your analysis, what do you do? I, for one, would probably be overly cynical about the state of bureaucracy, but don't fall into this trap with me. Focus on what you can change — a nice homage to the original Eightfold Path. Consider reaching out to a local representative or informing others around you, either via word of mouth or online. Realize that you won't always be able to change every policy, or the world for that matter, but being informed will serve you well.

Fast forward more than six years, and I can tell you why I marched in 2019 and, perhaps, why I shouldn't have marched; however, if I hadn't taken the time to learn what I was marching for, it would have been disengenuous to do so.
